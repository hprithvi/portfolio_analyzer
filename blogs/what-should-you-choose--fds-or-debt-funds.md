### What should you Choose — FDs or Debt Funds

In this post, we look at Fixed Deposits and Debt funds, compare their characteristics, and point out considerations for investors in making this choice.

FDs, the darling instrument of Indian public reigns supreme or Debt funds, the latest heartthrob?

Let’s first understand both in detail and then talk about suitability

FD, or Fixed Deposit, is simply, lending your money to a bank and the bank promising you to pay a certain amount of interest over the period that has been agreed upon (tenure). This money borrowed by the bank is then lent to their customers. Since you’re earning interest on the money you lent, the interest is considered as your income and is taxed at your highest slab rate. Other than the bank shutting doors, there are no risks in FD*.*Even in that scenario, FDs below INR 5 Lakh are insured. FDs have a tenure which dictates the interest rate you receive and come with a penalty on premature withdrawal (though some banks are offering no penalty withdrawals now). There are other details that determine your rate of return — your age (senior citizens are offered higher rates sometimes), frequency of compounding(daily compounding gives the best returns).

Debt funds, on the other hand, are a little more complex. In essence, you’re allowing a fund house or an AMC (the one you invest in) to lend the money to *entities* for some *period of time*. The italicized words are consequential, as we’ll see. The entities borrowing the money could be corporations, government, or entities attached to the government. The period of time could vary from a day (overnight funds) to more than a decade (long term funds). There are several risks associated with

You should consider mainly three risks in debt funds

1. Credit risk — risk that the borrower can’t pay back the borrowed money
2. Liquidity risk — very few people are ready to buy your debt mutual fund units, driving down the price
3. Interest rate risk — rise in interest rate decreases bond prices and hence the value of your investment goes down proportionately

Risk essentially means there’s a downside and that could translate to loss in principal, as well.

Having looked at characteristics of FDs and Debt funds, now we dive into considerations in choosing one over the other:

1. Risk Aversion: if you’re not open to the possibility of incurring loss to your deployed capital, you should consider FDs over Debt funds. Although there are Debt funds (like overnight funds) that have little risk, there’s no reason to invest in those just from a risk aversion perspective.
2. Flexibility/Liquidity: Some debt funds (liquid funds) provide better liquidity at lower cost compared to FDs, as each Liquid fund allows withdrawal upto Rs. 50,000 on business days with no penalties. Those FDs that offer no penalty withdrawal, are at parity with liquid funds.
3. Returns: Returns for FD are fixed (there’s a reason it’s called “Fixed” deposit) at the beginning. This is not the case with Debt funds. While there’s a historical trend that can be observed for different types of debt funds, the returns are not fixed and can’t be guaranteed. Investors can take guidance from historical returns with an eye on the variability in returns. Investors should be able to compare a fixed return with a distribution of returns in making a decision with respect to return.
4. Tenure: Fixed deposits have a predetermined tenure. Although there’s an option to carryover just the principal or a combination of principal and interest received over to another FD, there’s no flexibility in tenure. Debt funds can be sold at any time matching with the tenure the investor has in mind.
5. Taxation: Although both FDs and Debt funds will be taxed at the highest slab rate for an investor, cash flows are different and they can potentially affect the net return (post tax return). In FDs, there’s usually a periodic payment of interest (quarterly/half-yearly/annually) that is taxed at the highest slab rate. In the case of Debt funds, taxation comes into effect only when the profits are realised (MF units are sold). For long period holdings, just from the perspective of taxation, and assuming the same annual gross (pre tax) returns offered by FD and a debt fund, post-tax returns from the debt fund are better as the capital gains are taxed only once.

Hope this summary of Fixed Deposits vs. Debt Funds helps investors considering these as potential investment options. It is highly recommended to be thorough with the requirements and select the product that closely matches your needs.
