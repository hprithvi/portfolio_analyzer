### Safe Haven Investing

This is a summary of what I am taking away from “Safe Haven” by Mark Spitznagel. I have always been a fan of Nassim Taleb and his BS filtering methods. When I came across a post where Taleb was reading the book, I got the better of my procrastination and finally picked it up from the shelf (I already had it). While the foreword by Taleb itself was a delight, the book was thoroughly enjoyable. Although, I must confess that Spitz’s style of writing isn’t as catchy and gripping as Taleb’s(It could also be for the fact that while Taleb’s ideas are illustrated by various anecdotes, (thought) experiments, and hard numbers, Spitz’s book is on risk management in investing and hence has lesser scope for weaving charm through words).

Let’s get started. Investors are always after “multi-baggers”, market-beating returns, and alpha-returns. Starting with [Saint Petersburg paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Petersburg_paradox#:~:text=to%20the%20participants.-,The%20St.,the%20paradox%20have%20been%20proposed.) (a modified version) this book demystifies these tall asks from investors and boils down investing to risk management and a cost-effective at that one! The most important insight one could be taking away from the book is that utility is proportional to log of wealth. This is not just a hypothesis that is useful for arm-chair economists. It is the truth of investing. The result of investing in one cycle (a day, month, or a year) goes as an input for investment in the next cycle. Take the modified Petersburg paradox (numbers are different from the ones in the book) as an example here. A fair die (6 sided) is rolled and the number which appears on the die determines the outcome of a bet which costs ₹ 1,00,000 to enter. Below is a table illustrating the same.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*vH_UEWTPZXBH1SvUnK6COA.png)

As there is equal probability of getting each of these outcomes, do you think the price of the bet (₹1,00,000) is fair? How could we answer such a question? The *potential* value of the final/ending wealth should be at least equal to the price of the bet, or break-even. What is the *potential value* of the bet, you ask? Let us look at two of the means we know — arithmetic and geometric means. Geometric mean for the above example would be ₹1,13,323 and the arithmetic mean is ₹ 4,33,443. Which of these could be considered as the potential value? We have an excellent analogy to decode this. But some philosophy first.

The author speaks about how Neitzsche wrote about a demon which when asks a person if all the events of their life were to repeat for eternity without a change, would they be terrified or delighted? While Neitzsche believed that this is in fact the case, that our lives are lived repeatedly in the same manner for eternity, just tricking our brains into believing that that is indeed the case, gives some meaning to our lives. Think about it — if you had to take a decision *knowing that the consequences of the decision are to be borne repeatedly for eternity,* would you take it lightly? This is the case where you travel one and only one path repeatedly.  
Now consider another demon — Schrodinger’s demon. In Schrodinger’s quantum world, every action leads to a split in the universe leading to multiple universes *branching out.* So, every action is like a roll of the die and leads to a split in the universe depending on the number of possible outcomes. In the universe of Schrodinger’s demon, one can experience the consequences of *all possible outcomes, all at once*. So, a person rolling a die would experience all the six outcomes listed above.

Which of the two demons’ universe do you think is close to reality? Neitzsche’s or Schrodinger’s? Personally, I haven’t experienced the consequences of all the possible outcomes, all at once (I guess no one ever has). So, it’s safe to assume that we live in a Neitzsche’s demon’s universe of a single outcome (not sure about eternal repetition, though). If the analogy isn’t clear already, Arithmetic mean is analogous to Schrodinger’s demon’s universe and the Geometric mean to Neitzsche’s demon’s universe.

So, in the above example, the geometric mean of ₹1,13,323 is the better candidate for the potential outcome of the bet. Since it’s greater than the price to enter the bet (₹1,00,000), this bet is attractive for the player.

How is this relevant to investing, you might think. Returns or final wealth maximization is the goal of investing. Because wealth compounds, it is necessary that we use the right metric to measure it. Geometric return, hence, is the metric that we should maximize. However, markets are also prone to large losses which can effectively wipe out the wealth created over a period of time. How do we account for it? What measure could be used to asses it? Visualize this: You have a time horizon for investing, say, 25 years. Annual returns of your portfolio could range from say, -20% to +30%. The end wealth depends on the actual annual returns in each year. If a majority of years have negative returns or if the value of negative returns is quite high in a few years, effectively eroding your wealth, the final wealth could be less than what you started with resulting in negative final returns. On the other hand, the final wealth could be more than the initial wealth because of the positive returns outweighing the negative returns over the investing horizon. So, there’s a spectrum of possibilities of final returns(wealth) — a distribution. We have already established that the right metric to measure the returns is a geometric mean. Now, in the distribution of returns, median (50th percentile) is taken as the proxy for our target i.e., geometric mean returns. To also get an idea of the worst case scenario, 5th percentile returns are taken as the proxy. (The choice of these two proxies, though not perfect, is not arbitrary. With the distributions of both final returns and final wealth skewed, median makes a good candidate for central tendency and 5th percentile a candidate for an unfavorable outcome). An interesting and intuitive way to look at median and 5th percentile returns is to think of them as accuracy and precision. Median is like accuracy- it’s concerned with the target, not the distribution around it. Precision talks about how close the values are, without any regard to the value around which that distribution is present.

A small detour to model real world portfolio returns using the dice example:

- Instead of a 6-sided die that represents 6 possible outcomes, we can have an n-sided die representing n possible outcomes(although the author points out that the largest n-sided regular polygon that could be built is a 120-sided one which luckily coincides with the 120 yearly returns data available for S&P Index). The n-sides here represent the returns for n equal time periods (yearly returns are preferred for the want of stability). Let’s split these returns into ‘k’ number of buckets. For example, if the returns range from -20% to +40%, we can split the whole range into 4 buckets, each with a length of 15%.
- Instead of the given condition that the die was fair (implying equal probability of all outcomes), we will have a frequency distribution of outcomes in the buckets. For example, if a returns of 10–15% occurs with a frequency of 20 out of a total frequency of all outcomes of 100, the 10–15% bucket has a probability of 0.2. Likewise for all the buckets. (The division of buckets should be done by looking at the range of returns)
- For each bucket, we multiply the relative frequency and the returns to get the final returns.

Safe Havens have two properties:

- They protect wealth during ‘financial storms’
- They give higher returns in a cost-effective way

Safe Havens act like insurance during rough times. They give positive payoffs during bad times but the issue with insurance is it seems almost useless during good times. This is the part an investor should be perfectly okay with. Small regular cuts to wealth in exchange for protection against a huge blow. Bring the idea of geometric mean and log here. One big blow, say, to the tune of 40% wipes out a huge amount of wealth (making us incapable of investing it further) and brings down the final returns drastically. That one big blow is what we need to guard against. That in itself is a win. Someone might argue, “Well, to limit the downside, I’ll invest all my wealth in risk-free products or carry cash”. The downside to this is the opportunity cost of investing in markets and getting good returns during good times. This is the cost of risk management. Safe haven investing maximizes the returns at minimal cost.

Taleb writes in Black Swan that our anatomy is not tuned to bear small and continuous pain. It’s a nuisance. However, we require regular doses of ‘happiness’, however small the amount may be. The opposite of this is exactly what one needs to practice in investing. Be ready for small cuts and protect against big losses.

Now, we have a framework to build a real-life scenario from the dice game that we had discussed. In the framework, we only spoke about investing in markets/index funds. As candidates for Safe haven investing, we have an Fixed income portfolio and an insurance portfolio, through intuition. Insurance and Fixed income portfolios are where part of the wealth is invested in insurance and fixed income products respectively. For example, an insurance portfolio could be something like this — 90% of wealth invested in index funds and 10% in insurance. This insurance gives a payout only during an adverse event (say, market falls by >10%) and no payout otherwise. Let’s call this Insurance-Index Portfolio. Similarly, a fixed income portfolio could be something like this — 80% wealth invested in index funds and 20% wealth invested in a fixed returns product that pays out, say, 7% interest irrespective of market returns. Let’s call this Fixed-Index portfolio

What fraction of wealth should be allocated to insurance and fixed income products in each case? What allocation would maximize the final wealth? What should be maximized? The arithmetic average (mean) returns? The median returns? The worst possible case (say, 5th percentile)? We have already seen how mean returns could be misleading. There is a nice analogy in the book about median and 5th percentile returns. Median is analogous to accuracy — how close are we to the target (say, in darts, closer to the centre). 5th percentile is analogous to precision — how close are the darts to each other? When we talk about cost effective risk mitigation, we should try to maximize one *at some level of the other*. Here, we shall fix 5th percentile returns and try to maximize median returns.

First, let us build three scenarios: (1) All wealth in Index funds (2) Fixed-Index portfolio (3) Insurance-Index portfolio. We’ll try to plot median and 5th percentile returns for these three portfolios with varying allocations of wealth to fixed returns and insurance products in portfolios 2 and 3. For all the three portfolios, we consider the following: Investment horizon is 25 years, Nifty 50 annual returns (from the inception of the index; Taken from [NSE website](https://www1.nseindia.com/products/content/equities/indices/historical_index_data.htm)) are taken as the payoffs, Number of iterations(to construct a distribution in each portfolio) is 20,000, 6% returns in the case of Fixed-Index portfolio(this is a reasonable assumption, higher, in fact, as per the current savings rates), 10 times the insurance premium payoff when an adverse event of >10% fall in index returns occurs in the case of Insurance-Index portfolio. (The script for building these three portfolios can be found through the link in the appendix.

It is interesting to observe here that the fifth percentile and median returns for Insurance-Index portfolio (15.79% and 9.54% respectively) are much higher than those for Index only or Fixed-Index portfolios. By allocating a small amount of our wealth to insurance, we have not only increased accuracy (median returns) but also precision (5th percentile). The Insurance-Index portfolio satisfies the two properties of a Safe haven — protecting wealth and giving higher returns at a lower cost.
