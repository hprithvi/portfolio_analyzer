**Asset Classes**

An asset class is a set of assets that share similarity in one or more of the following —  
- *Their structure* (Eg: Stocks represent fractional ownership in a firm while debt doesn’t translate to ownership under normal circumstances)  
- *The factors affecting their returns & risk* (Eg: Credit risk is a common type of risk for fixed income assets)  
- *Regulatory concerns/rules* (Eg: SEBI, the investment regulatory body of India has prescribed some rules for dealing in equity instruments)  
- *Liquidity* (Eg: Real Estate is relatively illiquid compared to, say, stocks)

**What asset classes are there?**

1. Equity — Stocks (held in public or private companies), Equity Mutual Funds,
2. Fixed Income— Debt Mutual Funds, Bonds, Commercial Paper
3. Commodities — Gold, Silver, Coffee, Cotton etc.
4. Real Estate — Land, Buildings, Offices etc.
5. Cash and Cash equivalents — Money market funds, Fixed Deposit, Recurring Deposit etc.
6. Alternate investments — Collectibles (think of vintage cars, decades old whiskey etc.), NFTs

**Why so many asset classes?**

Each asset class has different characteristics that appeals to or aligns with needs of different set of investors. It is important to understand the characteristics of each asset class to evaluate if those characteristics meet an investor’s needs. That said, an asset class is not homogeneous in the sense that there are multiple products in each asset class with varying degrees of the characteristics of the asset class it belongs to.

Deep Dive into Equity Asset Class:

1. Stocks — Simply put, fractional ownership in a company. Say there are 100 shares issued by a company BIG BULL Pvt. Ltd. and you own 10 of those. You own 10% of the company.   
   (the word share is synonymously used with the word stock)  
   There are nuances in this worth noting.   
   Class A shares are those with voting rights. Mark Zuckerberg owns a majority of class A shares of Meta even though he doesn’t own a majority of equity. This allows him to retain control of making decisions despite being a minority shareholder. Usually, the shares traded on the market come with voting rights.  
   Then, there are preferential shares. These have a preferential status over the regular stocks in two aspects. Firstly, if any dividends are declared, the preferential share holders must be compensated before the dividends are distributed with regular shareholders. Secondly, in case of liquidation of a company, the preferential share holders must be compensated before the regular shareholders get any piece of the liquidated assets.
2. Mutual Funds  
   There are thousands of stocks across sectors and choosing the right set of stocks is not easy for retail investors. Mutual Funds solve this problem by allowing an investor to invest in a basket of stocks (and fixed income instruments, as well).   
   Let’s see how this works through an overly simplified example. Say there are three companies — Big Bull Ltd., Big Bear Ltd, and Big Sloth Ltd. An Asset Management Company or AMC, wishes to offer a basket of these three stocks in the form of Mutual Funds for investors. Say, the ratio in which the three stocks are offered is 3:2:1. This AMC, let’s call it Tiger Mutual Fund, buys 3 shares of Big Bull, 2 shares of Big Bear, and 1 share of Big Sloth and converts into 1 unit of Tiger Mutual Fund. Now, an investor can get fractional ownership of three companies just by buying one unit of Tiger Mutual Fund. This unit of mutual fund is bought and sold based on the closing prices of the underlying assets and they are not traded throughout the day.  
   Different Classification of Mutual Funds -

*Active vs Passive:*

Active funds are those that are managed by fund managers with certain degree of discretion. Passive funds usually track an index (say, Nifty 50).

Let’s continue with our mutual fund example and see the difference between active and passive funds. Let’s say there’s an index (let’s call it Top 3 Companies Index) that tracks the companies in the market. Let’s say this index is comprised of Big Bull, Big Bear, and Big Sloth Ltd. companies in the ratio of 3:2:1. A mutual fund that tracks this index is said to be a passive fund. In this case, one unit of the mutual fund has to correspond to 3 shares of Big Bull, 2 shares of Big Bear, and 1 share of Big Sloth companies. From the earlier example, Tiger Mutual Fund is a passive fund. Passive funds are also called Index Funds because they track an index.

If, on the other hand, there’s another mutual fund (let’s call it Elephant Mutual Fund) that wants to tweak this 3:2:1 ratio or wants to invest in some other company called Big Cat Ltd. Then it will not be tracking Top 3 companies index (that is comprised of 3 shares of Big Bull, 2 shares of Big Bear, and 1 share of Big Sloth companies). In this case, Elephant Mutual Fund is an active fund. While Active funds don’t track an index, they are benchmarked against an index. For example, Elephant Fund could have Top 3 Companies Index as its benchmark.

*Fund Classification based on Asset Class(es) invested in*

Mutual funds can invest in more than one asset class. This gives different types of mutual funds based on the underlying asset classes

An Equity fund is one that invests at least 65% of its funds in Equity and related instruments. This means that the rest 35% could be held across other asset classes like fixed income, cash, or real estate. This type of fund has further sub-classification based on the capitalization of the companies it majorly invests in – Large Cap, MidCap, Small Cap, Mid and Small Cap funds. For example, Sundaram Large Cap Fund is an Equity type mutual fund with its predominant investments going to large cap companies.

A Hybrid fund is one that invests across equity and fixed income asset classes. Think of it this way – a part of your investment in this fund would give you fractional ownership of a company while the remaining part of your investment is lent as debt. There are sub-classifications in Hybrid funds based on the percentage allotted to equity and fixed income asset classes.   
 — An Equity oriented hybrid fund invests a major part in equity (~ 65%) and the rest in debt/fixed income asset class.   
 — A conservative hybrid fund invests mostly in debt (75–90%) and the rest in equity. This is closer to a pure debt fund than an equity fund  
 — An aggressive hybrid fund can invest 75 to 90% in equity and the rest in fixed income  
 — A Multi-asset allocation fund invests in at least three asset classes (say, equity, debt, and gold)   
 — A Dynamic asset allocation fund can invest anywhere in the spectrum of 100% debt and no equity to 100% equity and no debt  
 — An Arbitrage fund generates returns by implementing arbitrage opportunities in different asset classes

A Debt or a Fixed Income fund invests in debt asset class. A simplified way to think about debt funds is that your investment is used to purchase bonds, commercial paper, or other products all of which could be thought of as equivalent to your money being lent for different time periods ranging from 1 day (in the case of overnight funds), 3 months (liquid funds), ultrashort term (< 1 year), to long term (10+ years) to different borrowers — corporates, banks, governments, municipalities, or for special purposes (building highways and the like)

3. ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds):  
ETFs are similar to mutual funds in the sense that they represent a unit of a (mixture of) some underlying assets. That’s where the similarity ends. Unlike mutual funds that are managed by AMCs, ETFs usually have sponsors who convert a set of underlying assets into units of a ETF. Let’s go back to the example we used for mutual funds. Say there are three companies — Big Bull Ltd., Big Bear Ltd, and Big Sloth Ltd. The ETF sponsor creates an ETF unit in exchange for 3 shares of Big Bull, 2 shares of Big Bear, and 1 share of Big Sloth from an Authorized Participant (AP) — large scale brokers and institutional investors. This ETF unit is now available for purchase in the secondary market by investors and traded during the market hours.

4. Fund of Funds:  
This is a mutual fund that invests in other funds instead of assets directly. For example, one Giraffe Mutual Fund that invests in Tiger Mutual Fund (a passive one) and Elephant Mutual Fund (an active one) could be called a Fund of Funds.

Dimensions of Investing —

Returns: A rather simple word yet this word hides nuance that is worthwhile giving attention to.  
Returns refers to how much you have earned relative to the amount you’ve invested. There are a few ways to look at this:

*Absolute Returns*: The difference between the final value and initial value as a percentage of the initial value.  
Eg: You invested Rs. 100 1 year 3 months ago and the value of that investment now is Rs. 120. Absolute returns in this case is (120–100)/100 = 20%. Note that there is no importance given to the time that has passed ( 1 year 3 months) or the possibility of investments and withdrawals that could have happened in that period. Absolute returns is a good metric to use when the time period is less than 1 year.

*Annualized Return*: The more popular name CAGR (Compounded Annual Growth Rate) is the rate at which the investment has changed taking into account the time component and assuming the growth compounds. It is calculated as (Final Value/Initial Value)^(1/Time) -1  
Too much jargon? Let’s jump into an example (the same scenario in Absolute Returns)  
Eg: You invested Rs. 100 1 year 3 months ago and the value of that investment now is Rs. 120. CAGR would be (120/100)^(1/1.25) -1 =15.7%   
(Since we’re considering annualized return (the A in CAGR), we need to convert months to years, 3 months = 0.25 years. 15 months = 1.25 years; Final Value is 120, Initial Value is 100).   
CAGR is a better metric over absolute returns if the time period exceeds 1 year.

*IRR & XIRR*: (Extended) Internal Rate of Return is the rate at which all the cashflows should be discounted for the Net Present Value (NPV) of the investment to be zero. Let’s break this jargon-filled sentence. Cashflow is literally that — flow of cash — either from your account or into your account. Net Present Value is the *Net* (positives minus negatives) Value at *Present* (as if all the cashflows are happening at present) time. What is meant by ‘*as if’* at present time? Let’s take an example. You invest Rs. 100 today and after 1 year 3 months expect the value of that investment to be Rs. 120.
